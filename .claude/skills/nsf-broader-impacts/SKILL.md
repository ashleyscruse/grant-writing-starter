# NSF Broader Impacts

Draft a Broader Impacts (BI) section that an NSF panel scores well: a specific, credible, assessable plan for the societal benefit of the work, grounded in the researcher's real record rather than generic good intentions. This is one of NSF's two review criteria, weighted equally with Intellectual Merit, and weak BI sinks otherwise strong proposals.

This skill is a specialization of `grant-writing`. All of that skill's voice and style rules apply here (evidence-based claims, SMART objectives, outputs vs outcomes, asset-based framing, no em dashes, no fabricated data). This file adds what is specific to NSF Broader Impacts.

## Before drafting

Read, in this order:
- `grants/{name}/solicitation.md` — the program's specific BI expectations and any program-specific broadening-participation priorities. Mirror its language.
- `profile/me/teaching-mentoring.md` — the researcher's real mentoring counts, programs led, courses, and outreach. **This is the evidence base for BI.** Use actual numbers and named programs, not invented activities.
- `profile/me/research.md` and `profile/institutions/` — institutional context (HBCU/MSI mission, existing pipelines, partnerships) that makes the plan credible.

If `teaching-mentoring.md` is still a blank template, say so and point the researcher to fill it (or to Sage) before drafting. Do not invent outreach the researcher has not done.

## What a strong BI section contains

1. **The societal benefit, stated plainly.** What changes for whom, connected to one or more of NSF's broad goals (full participation of groups underrepresented in STEM, improved STEM education and educator development, public engagement, broadening participation, partnerships, national security, economic competitiveness, infrastructure). Pick the goals the work genuinely serves; do not list all of them.
2. **A concrete activity plan.** Specific activities, audiences, numbers reached, timeline, and the partners or programs that carry them. Tie activities to the researcher's existing infrastructure where possible (it reads as feasible, not aspirational).
3. **Integration with the research.** Show BI is built into the project, not bolted on. The strongest sections make the research and its broader impact reinforce each other.
4. **An assessment plan.** How each activity's outcomes are measured, by whom, and against what baseline. Distinguish outputs (workshops held, students mentored) from outcomes (changes in participation, skills, or trajectories).
5. **Capacity and credibility.** Brief evidence the researcher and institution can deliver: prior BI track record, relevant partnerships, institutional support.

## Common ways BI loses points (avoid)

- Vague good intentions with no activities, numbers, audiences, or timeline.
- Activities with no assessment, or assessment that only counts outputs.
- "Broader impact" that is really just dissemination (publishing papers, presenting at conferences) with nothing added.
- Deficit framing of communities. Use asset-based, strengths-based language.
- Boilerplate that could appear in anyone's proposal. It must be specific to this researcher and this project.

## Output

Draft into `grants/{name}/proposal/` (the BI content belongs in the Project Summary's BI statement and the dedicated Broader Impacts portion of the Project Description). Map every claim to the researcher's profile and the solicitation's stated priorities. Then check it against `grant-writing`'s reviewer-perspective reference: would a skeptical panelist find it specific, feasible, and assessable?

## What this skill does NOT do

- Write Intellectual Merit or the research plan (that is the Project Description skill / `grant-writing`).
- Invent mentoring, outreach, or partnerships the researcher has not done.
- Judge fit or eligibility (that is `grant-analyzer`).
