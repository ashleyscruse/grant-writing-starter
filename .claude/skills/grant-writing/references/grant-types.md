## Federal Grants

### Characteristics
- Highly structured and prescriptive: strict formats, page limits, compliance requirements, specific forms (SF-424, etc.)
- Data-driven and evidence-based: prioritize measurable outcomes, validated instruments, alignment with federal policy objectives
- Formal review process: points-based scoring systems with expert panels
- Long preparation timelines: 80 to 100+ hours per application
- Funding may arrive 6 to 12 months after submission
- Matching funds often required; many are reimbursement-based
- Funded by taxpayer dollars: must serve public policy objectives and demonstrate public benefit

### Writing Conventions
- Mirror the language of the RFP/RFA/NOFO exactly
- Use section headers that match the funder's evaluation criteria
- Include citations from peer-reviewed research and government data sources
- Provide specific metrics, validated instruments, and statistical methods
- Write to the scoring rubric: address every criterion, in order, with explicit language
- Assume reviewers will score sections independently

### NIH Simplified Peer Review Framework (Effective January 2025)
Three evaluation factors replace the former five criteria:
1. **Factor 1, Importance of the Research** (combines Significance + Innovation): Scored 1-9
2. **Factor 2, Rigor and Feasibility** (based on Approach): Scored 1-9. Approach has the highest correlation with overall impact score
3. **Factor 3, Expertise and Resources** (combines Investigator + Environment): Evaluated as sufficient or not sufficient (binary). Designed to reduce reputational bias

### Common Federal Funders
- NIH (standard due dates: February, June, October)
- NSF
- Department of Education
- USDA
- HHS (HRSA, SAMHSA, ACF)
- Department of Justice (OJP, OVW)
- EPA
- Department of Labor

## Foundation Grants

### Characteristics
- Narrative-driven: shorter, more flexible proposals emphasizing mission alignment, storytelling, and values
- Relationship-centered: program officers often engage in pre-award conversations; relationship building matters
- Holistic review: mission alignment, organizational capacity, and community impact weighed alongside data
- Faster decisions: typically 2 to 6 months from submission to decision
- More flexible budgets: less rigid cost categories, minimal matching requirements, advance payments common
- Growing sector: over one-third of U.S. foundations expect to increase giving in 2025-2026
- Trend toward general operating support and trust-based philanthropy

### Writing Conventions
- Lead with story and mission alignment, supported by data
- Use the foundation's language and reference their strategic priorities
- Shorter proposals (often 5 to 15 pages)
- More conversational tone, but still precise and evidence-supported
- Emphasize impact stories alongside metrics
- Show how the project advances the foundation's specific theory of change

### Pre-Award Strategy
- Research the foundation's giving history, board members, and strategic plan
- If the foundation accepts LOIs (letters of inquiry), use them to test fit before investing in a full proposal
- Build relationships with program officers before applying when possible
- Attend funder briefings, webinars, and convenings

## Corporate Grants

### Characteristics
- Aligned with corporate social responsibility (CSR) and brand strategy
- Often require visibility and recognition: naming opportunities, event sponsorship, logo placement
- Shorter proposals: often 2 to 5 pages
- Employee engagement tie-ins: volunteer opportunities, skills-based partnerships, matching gifts
- Faster turnaround but typically smaller amounts
- Focused on community impact within the company's geographic footprint or strategic interest areas

### Writing Conventions
- Emphasize mutual benefit: how the project advances the company's CSR goals
- Include recognition and visibility opportunities explicitly
- Highlight employee engagement possibilities
- Keep proposals concise and business-oriented
- Use the company's CSR language and priorities
- Include clear deliverables and reporting commitments

## Congressional Directed Spending / Community Project Funding

### Characteristics
- Proposals go to Senators and House Members, not federal agencies
- House members capped at 15 requests each; competition is intense
- Community support documentation is mandatory: letters from local stakeholders, resolutions from governing bodies
- Projects must be tied to existing federal authorization law and eligible program accounts
- No for-profit recipients allowed
- Preparation should begin 6 to 12 months before submission deadlines
- Constituent submission windows typically January through February
- Congressional deadlines to Appropriations Committee typically April through May

### Writing Conventions
- Demonstrate broad community support through diverse letters and resolutions
- Tie the project to specific federal program accounts and authorization language
- Show financial and administrative readiness equivalent to competitive federal grants
- Emphasize local economic impact, job creation, and community benefit
- Include specific cost estimates with supporting documentation
- Address how the project aligns with the member's stated priorities and district needs

### Required Documentation
- Detailed project description with scope, timeline, and deliverables
- Letters of support from community stakeholders, elected officials, and beneficiaries
- Resolutions from local governing bodies (city council, county commission)
- Evidence of matching funds or organizational investment
- Certification that no federal lobbying funds were used
- Documentation of community engagement and public benefit
