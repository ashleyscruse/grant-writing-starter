## Executive Summary / Abstract

Write this section last but position it first. It must stand alone as a complete overview.

### Structure
1. One sentence identifying the applicant and project name
2. One to two sentences on the problem and who it affects
3. Two to three sentences on the proposed solution and key activities
4. One sentence on expected outcomes with specific numbers
5. One sentence on total funding requested and project duration

### Rules
- Stay under one page (often 250 words for federal grants)
- Include every key element: who, what, where, when, why, how much
- Use the funder's language and priority keywords
- Do not introduce information absent from the full proposal
- Write in third person unless the funder specifies otherwise

## Statement of Need

Center the community, not the organization. The need belongs to the people the project will serve.

### Data Requirements
- Use data from the last 3 to 5 years from reputable sources (Census, BLS, CDC, peer-reviewed studies, community needs assessments)
- Combine national trend data with local and regional data specific to the service area
- Include both quantitative evidence (statistics, demographic data, trend lines) and qualitative evidence (surveys, interviews, community voices)
- Cite every data point with source and year

### Community Voice
- Include direct quotes from beneficiaries, survey results, or testimony from people the project will serve
- Reference community engagement activities: listening sessions, advisory boards, focus groups, needs assessments
- Show that the community defined the problem, not just the organization

### Framing
- Use asset-based language that acknowledges community resilience and capacity
- Avoid deficit-based framing that portrays communities as broken or helpless
- Name root causes and systemic factors, not just symptoms
- Connect the need directly to the proposed solution (the need statement should make the project feel inevitable)

### Structure
1. Open with a compelling, specific data point or community voice
2. Establish the scope and severity of the problem nationally
3. Narrow to the local context with area-specific data
4. Introduce contributing factors and root causes
5. Show what happens if the problem goes unaddressed
6. Transition to the proposed solution

## Goals, Objectives, and Outcomes

### Definitions
- **Goal**: Broad, long-term change the project contributes to (not fully achievable within the grant period alone)
- **Objective**: Specific, measurable target achievable within the grant period
- **Output**: What the project produces or delivers (number of trainings, people served, materials created)
- **Outcome**: What changes as a result (behavior change, skill gain, improved indicators)

### SMART Framework
Every objective must be:
- **Specific**: Who, what, where
- **Measurable**: A number, percentage, or observable indicator
- **Achievable**: Realistic given the budget, timeline, and team
- **Relevant**: Directly connected to the stated need and funder priorities
- **Time-bound**: Includes a deadline or timeframe

### Common Mistakes
- Confusing outputs with outcomes (delivering 10 workshops is an output; 75% of participants demonstrating improved skills is an outcome)
- Setting unmeasurable objectives ("increase awareness" without specifying how awareness is measured)
- Overpromising behavior change timelines
- Writing objectives that cannot be evaluated with the proposed methods

### Example Format
Goal: Reduce food insecurity among families in [County] by improving access to nutritious food.

Objective 1: By [date], establish [number] new food distribution sites in [specific neighborhoods], serving [number] households per month.

Objective 2: By [date], [percentage] of participating families will report improved food security as measured by the USDA Household Food Security Survey Module.

## Project Description / Methodology

### Structure
1. Overview of the approach and why it was selected
2. Evidence base: cite research, models, or prior results supporting the approach
3. Detailed activities organized by objective or phase
4. Timeline with milestones and responsible parties
5. Target population: who will be served, how they will be recruited, and how barriers to participation will be addressed
6. Staffing: key personnel, roles, qualifications, and time commitment

### Evidence-Based Practice
- Cite the research or theoretical framework supporting the approach
- Reference prior implementations, pilot results, or preliminary data
- If the approach is innovative, explain why existing methods are insufficient and what evidence suggests this approach will work

### Timeline
- Use a table or Gantt chart format
- Include quarterly or monthly milestones
- Assign responsible parties to each activity
- Build in realistic ramp-up time for hiring, procurement, and partner coordination
- Account for seasonal factors, academic calendars, or other timing constraints relevant to the population

### Accessibility and Equity
- Describe how the project will reach underserved or historically excluded populations
- Address barriers to participation: transportation, language access, childcare, digital access, ADA compliance
- Use person-first language and community-preferred terminology

## Logic Model

### Components (left to right)
1. **Inputs/Resources**: Funding, staff, partners, facilities, equipment, existing data
2. **Activities**: What the project will do (trainings, outreach, service delivery, data collection)
3. **Outputs**: Direct products of activities (number of sessions, participants, materials distributed)
4. **Short-term Outcomes**: Changes within 1 to 2 years (increased knowledge, changed attitudes, new skills)
5. **Long-term Outcomes/Impact**: Changes at 3+ years (systemic change, policy impact, population-level indicators)

### Rules
- Every input must connect to at least one activity
- Every activity must produce at least one output
- Every output must link to at least one outcome
- If a connection is broken or missing, the model is flawed
- Use the W.K. Kellogg Foundation Logic Model Development Guide as a reference framework
- Present visually as a table, flow chart, or diagram
- Keep it to one page

### Common Mistakes
- Including outcomes that cannot be measured with the proposed evaluation plan
- Listing activities that have no corresponding budget line
- Skipping the short-term outcome level and jumping from outputs to long-term impact
- Overstating the causal chain (correlation is not causation)

## Evaluation Plan

### Mixed Methods Approach
Combine quantitative and qualitative methods:

**Quantitative**: Pre/post assessments, validated survey instruments, administrative data, comparison groups, dosage tracking

**Qualitative**: Interviews, focus groups, case studies, open-ended survey questions, observation protocols

### Required Components
1. **Evaluation questions**: What will the evaluation answer? (tied to each objective)
2. **Design**: Formative (ongoing improvement) and summative (final impact) components
3. **Indicators and metrics**: Specific, measurable indicators for each outcome
4. **Data sources and instruments**: Name the specific tools (e.g., PHQ-9 for depression screening, USDA Food Security Module)
5. **Data collection timeline**: When and how often data will be gathered
6. **Analysis methods**: How data will be analyzed (statistical tests, coding frameworks)
7. **Evaluator**: Who will conduct the evaluation (internal, external, or both) and why
8. **Reporting**: How findings will be shared with stakeholders, funders, and the community
9. **Participatory elements**: How community members will be involved in evaluation design and interpretation

### When to Use an External Evaluator
- Federal grants over $500K typically expect external evaluation
- When objectivity is critical for credibility
- When specialized methodological expertise is needed
- Always explain the rationale for the choice

## Organizational Capacity

### What to Demonstrate
- Relevant experience: similar projects completed, populations served, outcomes achieved
- Fiscal management: audit history, financial controls, experience managing federal or foundation funds
- Infrastructure: facilities, technology, systems for data collection and reporting
- Staff qualifications: key personnel credentials, relevant experience, and track record
- Governance: board composition, oversight structures, organizational stability

### Past Performance
- Cite specific projects with measurable results
- Reference prior grants managed successfully, especially from the same funder or similar agencies
- Include data on populations served and outcomes achieved
- If the organization is new, emphasize staff experience and partner capacity

## Sustainability Plan

### Required Elements
1. How the project will continue after grant funding ends
2. Specific revenue diversification strategies:
   - Fee-for-service or sliding-scale models
   - Recurring donor programs and major gift cultivation
   - Corporate sponsorships and in-kind partnerships
   - Additional grant funding from other sources
   - Earned income strategies
   - Government contracts or reimbursement mechanisms
3. Cost reduction over time: one-time investments (training, technology, curriculum) that do not recur, train-the-trainer models
4. Institutionalization: how methods, tools, or findings will be integrated into ongoing operations
5. Partnership commitments beyond the grant period
6. SMART milestones for sustainability (not just project outcomes)

### Framing
- Answer the funder's real question: "Is this a smart investment?"
- Show that sustainability planning begins at project launch, not in the final year
- Be specific about which elements will continue and how they will be funded
- Acknowledge what may not continue and why

## Budget and Budget Narrative

### Line Item Requirements
For each line item, provide:
- **Personnel**: Name or title, role, percentage of time/effort, salary basis, fringe benefit rate, and what each person will do
- **Equipment**: Justification for why each item is needed, why this option over less expensive alternatives, cost basis (vendor quotes, catalog prices)
- **Supplies**: Purpose, unit cost, quantity, total, and cost basis
- **Travel**: Purpose, destination, number of trips, number of travelers, per diem/mileage rates (use GSA rates for federal grants)
- **Contractual/Consultants**: Why outside expertise is needed, selection process, rate basis
- **Other**: Full justification for anything not in standard categories
- **Indirect costs**: Federally negotiated rate or funder's de minimis rate

### Rules
- Every narrative activity must have a corresponding budget line
- Every budget line must connect to a narrative activity
- Budget math must be error-free (double-check totals, subtotals, and percentages)
- Match the budget total in the spreadsheet to the total in the narrative and the total on the application form
- Do not pad: every position must have a clear role, every item a clear justification
- Account for matching/cost-share requirements if applicable
- Include in-kind contributions to demonstrate organizational investment

## Letters of Support and Partnerships

### Letters of Support
- Each letter must reference the specific project by name
- Each letter must describe the partner's specific role and contribution
- Each letter should include data points or keywords aligned with funder priorities
- Provide a draft to each supporter 4 to 6 weeks before the deadline
- Include letters from diverse stakeholder types: partner organizations, community leaders, elected officials, beneficiaries, subject matter experts
- Generic, copy-paste letters are a red flag for reviewers

### Memorandums of Understanding (MOUs)
Required components:
1. Names and descriptions of all partnering organizations
2. Background and purpose of the partnership
3. Specific roles and responsibilities of each party
4. Services and resources each party will provide
5. Duration and renewal terms
6. General terms, conditions, and dispute resolution
7. Signatures of authorized representatives with dates

Obtain all signatures before submission.

### Partnership Documentation Standards
- Show partnerships are substantive: concrete resource sharing, joint activities, data-sharing agreements, co-designed programming
- Include partner organizations' relevant credentials and experience
- Demonstrate that collaboration is genuine and ongoing, not created solely for this application
