# NSF Grant Types

Which NSF grant type a solicitation is changes what you write: the sections it emphasizes, the focus of the prose, and what reviewers weight. Identify the type from the solicitation, then draft to its rules below.

Every NSF proposal, whatever the type, has a Project Summary (Overview, Intellectual Merit, Broader Impacts) and a Project Description that addresses Intellectual Merit and Broader Impacts. Formatting (font, spacing, margins, page limits) always defers to the solicitation.

## Research

Supports a basic research project. Applicants: faculty, researchers, universities.

- **Emphasis:** the research plan, objectives and questions, methodology, and preliminary results that show feasibility.
- **Writing focus:** rigorous specific methods, clear objectives, how the work advances the field, evidence from prior results.
- **Reviewers weight:** Intellectual Merit most heavily, then Broader Impacts; within that, the rigor and feasibility of the approach.
- **Pitfalls:** vague methods, objectives that overreach the budget and timeline, no preliminary data, a thin Broader Impacts bolted on.

## Education and workforce

Improves STEM education and workforce development. Applicants: colleges, school districts, nonprofits.

- **Emphasis:** program or curriculum design, target participants and recruitment, activities, the evaluation plan, sustainability, and broadening participation.
- **Writing focus:** an evidence-based design, a clear participant pipeline, measurable outcomes, and a real evaluation plan tied to those outcomes.
- **Reviewers weight:** the design and its evidence base, the evaluation plan, broadening participation, and sustainability.
- **Pitfalls:** activities with no assessment, vague recruitment, counting outputs instead of outcomes, no sustainability.

## Center

Establishes a multi-investigator research center. Applicants: universities and consortia.

- **Emphasis:** an integrated center vision, the research themes and their synergy, the multi-institutional team, management and governance, education and knowledge transfer, and sustainability beyond the award.
- **Writing focus:** why a center is needed rather than separate grants, how the parts integrate into more than their sum, and how it is managed.
- **Reviewers weight:** the case for a center, the integration and synergy, management and governance, and broader impacts at scale.
- **Pitfalls:** reading like a bundle of unrelated projects, a weak management or governance plan, no sustainability.

## Infrastructure and instrumentation

Purchases equipment or builds research infrastructure. Applicants: universities and research institutions.

- **Emphasis:** the instrument or infrastructure and its capabilities, the range of research it enables across users, the need and justification, shared multi-user access, and a management and maintenance plan with institutional commitment.
- **Writing focus:** the breadth of research enabled, why current capacity is insufficient, who will use it, and how it is managed, maintained, and sustained.
- **Reviewers weight:** the merit of the research enabled, the demonstrated need, multi-user benefit, and the management plan.
- **Pitfalls:** justifying it for a single user, a weak management or maintenance plan, no institutional commitment, understating shared use.

## Fellowship and scholarship

Supports students and postdoctoral researchers. Applicants: undergraduate, graduate, and postdoctoral researchers (individual awards), or institutions running scholarship programs.

- **Emphasis (individual award):** the person's potential and record, the research and training plan, the mentoring and training environment, and the broader impacts of the individual.
- **Emphasis (institutional scholarship program):** the recruitment, support, and retention design for students, and its evaluation.
- **Writing focus:** for an individual, make the case for the person and the plan; for a program, make the case for the design and its evidence.
- **Reviewers weight:** for individuals, the candidate plus the plan plus potential; for programs, the design plus evidence plus broadening participation.
- **Pitfalls:** for individuals, a generic plan with no training component; for programs, no student-support or evaluation design.

## Career development

Supports investigators at a specific career stage (for example, early-career faculty). Applicants: early-career and mid-career faculty.

- **Emphasis:** an integrated research and education plan, a multi-year vision, the candidate's trajectory toward leadership, and departmental and institutional support.
- **Writing focus:** show research and education as one integrated plan, not two; articulate the long-term vision; make the institutional support concrete.
- **Reviewers weight:** the integration of research and education, the candidate's potential to become a leader, and the credibility of the plan.
- **Pitfalls:** research and education plans that do not connect, no clear vision, vague institutional support.

## Small business

Supports commercialization of research (SBIR/STTR). Applicants: startups and small businesses (the small business is the applicant, not the university).

- **Emphasis:** the innovation, the technical objectives and feasibility (Phase I), the commercial potential and market, the team, and for STTR the research-institution partnership.
- **Writing focus:** write to commercial impact, not academic framing; establish feasibility and a credible path to market.
- **Reviewers weight:** intellectual merit and innovation together with commercial potential; broader impacts here are largely economic.
- **Pitfalls:** academic framing instead of commercial, a weak market or commercialization case, no clear feasibility objectives.

## Conference and workshop

Supports scientific meetings. Applicants: professional societies and universities.

- **Emphasis:** the significance of the topic to the field now, the organizers, the participants and how recruitment broadens participation, the format and agenda, dissemination, and the expected outcomes or report.
- **Writing focus:** why the meeting matters and why now, who convenes and attends, how participation is broadened, and what comes out of it.
- **Reviewers weight:** value to the community and broadening participation, and the plan for organizing and disseminating results.
- **Pitfalls:** no participant recruitment or diversity plan, no dissemination or written outcome, a budget that does not match the participant-support plan.

## Planning

Develops future research collaborations or centers. Applicants: research teams. Funds the planning activities, not the research itself.

- **Emphasis:** the plan, the team and partners convened, the concrete planning activities and milestones, and the larger effort it leads to.
- **Writing focus:** a credible specific plan with deliverables and a timeline, who is brought together and why, and what the follow-on effort will be.
- **Reviewers weight:** the feasibility and credibility of the plan, the team and partnerships, and the potential of the larger effort.
- **Pitfalls:** writing it as a research proposal, planning activities with no deliverables, no clear path to the next step.

## Capacity building

Strengthens institutional research capacity. Applicants: colleges, minority-serving institutions, EPSCoR states.

- **Emphasis:** the institutional capacity gap, the plan to build durable capacity (people, infrastructure, culture), institutional commitment, sustainability, and the research and education the capacity enables.
- **Writing focus:** frame it as building lasting capacity, not a one-off project; make institutionalization and sustainability concrete.
- **Reviewers weight:** the capacity-building case and its durability, institutional commitment, and broader impacts on the institution or region.
- **Pitfalls:** framing it as a single research project, no institutionalization or sustainability, weak institutional commitment.
