# NSF Proposal Sections

Per-section guidance for the components an NSF proposal commonly includes. Which sections apply, and how they are weighted, depends on the grant type; see [grant-types.md](grant-types.md). Formatting (page limits, font, spacing, margins) always defers to the solicitation. Write in prose; render sections as LaTeX `\section`.

## Project Summary

One page, three clearly labeled parts: Overview, Intellectual Merit, and Broader Impacts. It must stand alone, in third person, with no citations. Reviewers read it first and it frames everything.

- Overview: the problem, the goal, and the approach in a few sentences.
- Intellectual Merit: how the work advances knowledge.
- Broader Impacts: the benefit to society.

Pitfalls: missing one of the three labeled parts (a proposal can be returned without review), marketing language, and claims not supported in the Project Description.

## Project Description

The main narrative, to the solicitation's page limit (often 15 pages). It must explicitly address both Intellectual Merit and Broader Impacts. Structure follows the solicitation; in general it covers the problem and its significance, the objectives or research questions, the approach and methodology (specific and rigorous, with preliminary results where available), the Broader Impacts (integrated, see below), Results from Prior NSF Support when applicable, and a timeline with milestones and responsibilities.

Reviewers weight rigor, feasibility, and how convincingly Intellectual Merit and Broader Impacts are made. Pitfalls: vague methods, no preliminary data, Broader Impacts bolted on, ignoring a stated review criterion.

## Broader Impacts

One of NSF's two review criteria, weighted equally with Intellectual Merit. A specific, credible, assessable plan for the societal benefit of the work, grounded in the researcher's real record (`profile/me/teaching-mentoring.md`), not generic good intentions.

- State the societal benefit plainly, tied to one or more NSF goals (full participation of groups underrepresented in STEM, improved STEM education and educator development, public engagement, broadening participation, partnerships, national security, economic competitiveness, infrastructure). Pick the goals the work genuinely serves.
- Give a concrete activity plan: activities, audiences, numbers reached, timeline, and the partners or programs that carry them. Tie to existing infrastructure so it reads as feasible.
- Integrate it with the research so the two reinforce each other.
- Include an assessment plan: how each activity's outcomes are measured, by whom, and against what baseline. Distinguish outputs (workshops held, students mentored) from outcomes (changes in participation, skills, trajectories).
- Add brief capacity and credibility: prior Broader Impacts record, relevant partnerships, institutional support.

Pitfalls: vague intentions with no activities, numbers, or timeline; assessment that only counts outputs; dissemination dressed up as Broader Impacts; deficit framing of communities; boilerplate that is not specific to this researcher and project.

## Results from Prior NSF Support

Required if any PI or co-PI has had NSF support (including a no-cost extension) in the past five years. For each award, give the award number, amount, and period, then the outcomes labeled under Intellectual Merit and Broader Impacts, the resulting publications, and how it relates to the proposed work. It counts toward the Project Description page limit.

Pitfalls: omitting it when required, not labeling Intellectual Merit and Broader Impacts, and listing outputs with no outcomes.

## Facilities, Equipment and Other Resources

The resources available to carry out the work: computing, labs, instrumentation, space, and partnerships. This is where you name shared or national compute (pull from the institution's facilities document). State what is available and how it supports the project. No pricing or quotes here; that belongs in the budget. Reviewers use it to judge feasibility.

Pitfalls: aspirational resources stated as available, and generic boilerplate not tied to the project.

## Data Management and Sharing Plan

Up to two pages (per the solicitation). Cover the data types and expected volume, the standards and metadata, storage and preservation during and after the award, access and sharing and the repository used, and any privacy or IRB constraints. Infrastructure language (storage, transfer) can come from the institution's data-management document; the data-specific parts are the researcher's.

Pitfalls: a "no data" hand-wave, no repository or sharing timeline, ignoring metadata standards for the field.

## Mentoring Plan

Required when the budget includes postdoctoral researchers. Up to one page. Describe the mentoring activities for the postdoc, the career development and training provided, and how mentoring is delivered and assessed.

Pitfalls: a generic plan, no assessment, and omitting it when postdocs are budgeted.

## Budget Justification

Prose justifying every line in the grant's `budget.xlsx`: personnel and effort, fringe, equipment, travel, participant support, other direct costs, and indirect costs. Every narrative activity has a budget line, and every line ties back to the narrative. Numbers must match the sheet and the forms. Defer format and limits to the solicitation.

Pitfalls: padding, lines with no narrative rationale, and totals that do not match the sheet.

## References Cited

The works cited in the Project Description. Give complete citations and a hyperlink to each work's DOI where known. List only what is cited. Cite only sources in the grant's `literature/` folder unless that folder is empty or the researcher asks you to find literature (see the sourcing rule in the skill).

Pitfalls: citing work not in the narrative, and citing anything not grounded in the `literature/` folder.

## Logic model (a tool, not a section)

A logic model is a figure (inputs to activities to outputs to outcomes), not an NSF section. Use it inside the evaluation or program design of a program/education proposal when it clarifies the causal chain, as a single figure. Every input connects to an activity, every activity to an output, and every output to an outcome; do not include outcomes you cannot assess.
