## Reviewer Reality

Reviewers are typically exhausted, overworked experts reading proposals outside their immediate expertise, often late at night. They have approximately 5 minutes to summarize a proposal's aims, approach, and significance to the panel.

What this means for your writing:
- If the reviewer cannot quickly articulate the project's story coherently, the proposal will score poorly
- The first page (or Specific Aims page for NIH) is often the only part read by all reviewers
- Reviewers read with a pencil, looking for reasons to say yes or no
- Dense, poorly organized text gets skimmed, not read
- Reviewers may read sections out of order or only read certain parts

## What Reviewers Reward

### Low Risk, High Return
Reviewers want confidence that funding will result in measurable impact, high-quality outputs, or real-world change. Demonstrate:
- A clear, well-defined problem
- An approach with evidence of effectiveness
- A team with demonstrated capacity
- Realistic scope matched to the budget and timeline

### Clarity of Purpose
- A research question or problem statement that can be stated in one sentence
- A logical thread from need to method to outcome
- No ambiguity about what will be done, by whom, and when

### Timeliness and Necessity
- Why this project matters now
- What has changed (new data, policy shifts, emerging needs) that makes this urgent
- What happens if the project is not funded

### Feasibility
- Preliminary data, pilot results, or proof of concept
- Detailed, realistic timelines with milestones
- Key personnel with relevant credentials and time committed
- Organizational infrastructure to support the work

### Impact
- Both immediate deliverables and long-term significance
- Clear connection between project outcomes and broader systemic change
- Potential for replication, scale, or influence on policy and practice

## Red Flags That Tank Scores

1. **Vague or unmeasurable objectives**: "Increase awareness" without specifying how awareness is measured
2. **Budget-narrative mismatch**: Activities described in the narrative with no corresponding budget line, or budget items with no narrative justification
3. **Generic letters of support**: Copy-paste letters that do not reference the specific project or the partner's role
4. **No preliminary data**: Proposing an approach with no evidence it works, no pilot results, and no theoretical basis
5. **Poor writing quality**: Typos, grammatical errors, passive voice, inconsistent terminology
6. **Failure to address risks**: No discussion of potential challenges, limitations, or mitigation strategies
7. **Solution looking for a problem**: Technology or program that is not clearly needed by the target population
8. **Overpromising**: Outcomes that cannot realistically be achieved within the proposed scope, budget, and timeline
9. **Internal inconsistency**: Goals in one section that contradict methods in another, or numbers that do not match across sections
10. **Noncompliance**: Exceeding page limits, wrong font, missing required sections, late submission

## Common Proposal Mistakes

### Process Mistakes
- Not following the funder's instructions (the number one reason for rejection)
- Submitting the same proposal to every funder without customization
- Insufficient funder research before writing
- Submitting at the last minute, increasing error rates
- Not having colleagues review the proposal before submission

### Content Mistakes
- Centering the organization's needs instead of the community's needs
- Confusing outputs (what you do) with outcomes (what changes)
- Writing goals that are not SMART
- Including personnel without clearly defined project roles
- Measuring activities instead of change in the evaluation plan
- Ignoring the sustainability question entirely
- Failing to demonstrate impact with data
- Using deficit-based language about the community

### Writing Mistakes
- Excessive passive voice
- Jargon without explanation for mixed-discipline reviewers
- Overly long sentences and paragraphs
- No visual hierarchy (missing headers, bold text, lists)
- Inconsistent terminology (using different terms for the same concept)
- Emotional, hyperbolic, or unsupported claims

## Writing for Scanability

Reviewers scan before they read. Design the document so the key points are visible at a glance.

### Formatting Strategies
- Use headers that match the funder's evaluation criteria
- Bold key phrases and findings (not entire sentences, just the critical words)
- Use bulleted and numbered lists for complex information
- Include figures, tables, charts, and diagrams to summarize data and timelines
- Leave generous white space around headers and key sections
- Keep paragraphs short (3 to 5 sentences maximum)
- Use topic sentences that state the point, then provide evidence

### One-Page Test
For every major section, ask: if a reviewer only reads the first sentence of each paragraph and the bold text, do they get the full picture? If not, restructure.

### Acronym Discipline
- Spell out every acronym on first use in every major section (reviewers may not read sections in order)
- Do not assume the reviewer knows field-specific abbreviations
- Limit the total number of acronyms; too many reduce readability
